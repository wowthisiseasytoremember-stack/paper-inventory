### The "Cheat Sheet" for Maps & Charts
*   **RULE 1 (The Cartographer/Publisher):** Identify who made the map (e.g., Rand McNally, Thomas Jefferys, US Coast and Geodetic Survey). Maps from well-known historical cartographers are highly prized.
*   **RULE 2 (Date & "Firsts"):** Find the copyright or printing date. Value increases significantly if the map shows a "first" (e.g., the first map to show a newly formed state, territory, or major railroad route). Mention the date prominently.
*   **RULE 3 (Hand-Colored vs. Printed):** Older maps (pre-1900) were often hand-colored. Look for slight imperfections or variations in the coloring that indicate it was done by hand, as this generally increases value over mass-produced chromolithographs.
*   **RULE 4 (Nautical Charts):** For coastal areas, look for detailed depth soundings, lighthouses, and compass roses. These appeal to both local historians and maritime collectors.
*   **RULE 5 (Condition/Foxing):** Check for "foxing" (brown spots from age), tears along fold lines (common in maps removed from atlases), and whether the map has been laid down on canvas or board.
